## What It Looks Like

A very brief, high-amplitude artifact time-locked to the TMS pulse. It is the direct recording of the electromagnetic field generated by the TMS coil in the EEG electrodes. In well-prepared high-sampling recordings, the visible TMS-pulse artifact can be shorter than 1-2 ms, but its apparent duration depends strongly on amplifier settings, anti-aliasing filters, and sampling frequency.

At lower sampling rates or with amplifier/filter settings that broaden the pulse, the artifact may appear longer than the physical TMS pulse. With very high sampling, the artifact is still not necessarily captured in its true shape because hardware filters can smear the waveform around the trigger.

## Timing Clues

- Begins at the pulse/trigger time, though hardware filtering may make it appear to start slightly before zero.
- Usually ends before the later decay component becomes visually dominant.
- In immediate TEP studies, separate "end of TMS-pulse artifact" from "back to baseline"; they are not the same event.

## Polarity And Coil-Direction Clues

The waveform can invert or change polarity when coil current direction is reversed, for example PA versus AP stimulation. If an alleged early neural component reverses exactly with pulse polarity, treat it as high-risk for residual pulse artifact.

## Single-Trial Versus Average Clues

The pulse artifact is highly time-locked and therefore remains prominent in averages unless removed/interpolated or suppressed by amplifier hardware. Its earliest samples may be clipped, saturated, or filter-broadened.

## Common Confusions

- TMS pulse artifact versus decay artifact: the pulse artifact is the initial high-amplitude electromagnetic artifact; decay is the longer return-to-baseline component that can emerge after the pulse artifact ends.
- TMS pulse artifact versus i-TEP: early responses within the first few milliseconds require explicit artifact-window, sampling, synchronization, and coil-direction controls.
- TMS pulse artifact versus recharge/discharge: stimulator recharge/discharge can appear later and should be tracked separately.

## Acquisition Checks

- Record with sufficiently high sampling frequency when early responses are an endpoint.
- Document amplifier, sampling frequency, anti-aliasing filter behavior if known, trigger timing, and pulse-removal/interpolation window.
- Compare artifact duration across intensity levels and current directions when possible.
- If the apparent pulse artifact extends substantially beyond the first few milliseconds, inspect amplifier recovery, recharge/discharge, decay artifact, lead configuration, electrode contact, and synchronization.

## What Not To Conclude

- Do not interpret a sub-2 ms deflection as neural without showing that it is not residual pulse artifact.
- Do not assume that higher sampling rate alone solves all early artifact problems; it mainly helps characterize and reduce the initial TMS-pulse artifact, while decay can persist.
